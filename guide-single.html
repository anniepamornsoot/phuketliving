Can foreigners own property in Thailand? The honest answer.

By Annie · Founder, Phuket Living 8 min read Updated October 2024

Most relocation guides give you the marketing version of this answer: "Yes, foreigners can own property in Thailand, here's how!" The reality is more nuanced — and more important.

Here's what you actually need to know before you sign anything in Thai. This guide is written from seven years of helping families relocate, and from watching a few of them make expensive mistakes that could have been avoided with one honest conversation.

The short version

Foreigners cannot own land in Thailand outright. They can own a building (a villa, a house) on land they don't own. They can own a condominium freehold, provided foreign ownership in that building stays under 49%. Everything else is some flavour of leasehold or corporate structure.

That's the framework. The rest is the detail — and the detail matters.

Freehold condos — the simplest path

If you want full ownership of your home and you don't need a garden, a freehold condo is the cleanest legal path for a foreigner in Thailand. You hold the title in your own name, you can sell freely, and your heirs inherit it without complication.

The catch: each condo development has a 49% foreign ownership cap. If a building is already at the cap, you'll either need to wait for a foreign-owned unit to come up for resale, or look at a different development. We always check the cap status before recommending a unit.

What to verify before buying a condo

  • The current foreign quota status (request a written confirmation from the juristic person)
  • That the developer holds the land title — not a related company
  • Sinking fund and common-area fee history
  • Outstanding repairs or assessments

Leasehold villas — the most common path for families

Most expat families who want a villa with a garden end up on a leasehold structure. The standard is a 30-year lease, with two further 30-year renewal options written into the contract. In practice, this gives you 90 years of usage rights — long enough that for most life decisions, it's effectively ownership.

"The 30+30+30 structure isn't legally guaranteed beyond the first 30 years — but a properly written lease, with the right developer, makes it about as close to ownership as a foreigner can get without setting up a company."

The renewals aren't legally bulletproof — Thai law only enforces the first 30-year term. The strength of the renewals depends on the lease wording, the landowner's reliability, and how the contract is registered. We always recommend a Thai property lawyer review the lease before signing.

Company structures — proceed with caution

You'll see properties advertised as "company freehold" or sold via a Thai limited company. The structure works like this: a Thai company (with majority Thai shareholders, by law) owns the land, and you control the company through a director's agreement and class-A shares with voting rights.

This can be a legitimate structure — many businesses operate this way — but using it purely to bypass the foreign land ownership rules is a grey area, and the Thai government has periodically tightened enforcement. If you're considering this route:

  • Use a reputable Thai law firm (not the developer's "preferred" lawyer)
  • Make sure the Thai shareholders are real, not nominees
  • Understand the annual compliance cost (filings, audits, accountant)
  • Be prepared for the structure to be challenged at some point in the future

For most families, leasehold is simpler, cheaper to maintain, and almost as good in practice.

What to watch out for

A few patterns we see repeatedly with families who get burned:

  • Off-plan purchases from new developers without a track record — the deposit is rarely recoverable if the project stalls
  • Verbal promises about renewals — if it's not written into the lease and registered at the land office, it doesn't exist
  • Using the developer's lawyer — always engage your own independent counsel
  • Buying before renting — most regrets we hear are from families who bought after a two-week scouting trip

Annie's honest recommendation

For most families I work with: rent for 6–12 months first, in the area you think you want. If you still want to buy after that — and you've experienced both the dry and wet seasons — then a leasehold villa or a freehold condo is almost always the right structure. Company structures are rarely worth the complexity for residential use.
